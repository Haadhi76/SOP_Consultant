# Move framework, appeals, and self-construction

Read this before outlining or drafting an SOP. It contains:

1. The move/step taxonomy
2. How the moves are sequenced in real successful SOPs
3. The three rhetorical appeals (ethos, logos, pathos) and how to weight them
4. Self-construction — building coherent "selves"

The taxonomy comes from Samraj & Monk (2008), as coded and extended by Ganguly
(2020); the appeals analysis is Aristotelian (logos/pathos/ethos) as applied to
SOPs in the same research.

---

## 1. The move/step taxonomy

A "move" is a chunk of the SOP doing one rhetorical job; "steps" are the ways to
realize it. Not every step is needed in every SOP — pick what the applicant's
material and the program type call for.

**Move 1 — Introduction**
- *Generalization*: open on a specific personal, professional, or discipline
  experience (see style guidance: avoid the grand abstract opener).
- *Background*: a brief snapshot of where the applicant is coming from.
- *Goals / decision to apply*: signal the purpose. This can appear later than
  paragraph one — it does not have to be front-loaded.

**Move 2 — Establishing Credentials (Background)**
- *General* (family / formative experience): use only if it genuinely explains
  the path; do not pad.
- *Work*: prior professional experience and its relevance to the target. When
  present, applicants tend to lean on this heavily — it is strong ethos.
- *Education*: academic background and how it connects to the target field.
  This is the most common credibility anchor.
- *Research*: prior research, where relevant (essential for PhD, optional for
  taught master's).
- *Personal Attributes*: qualities — but only when tied to evidence. An
  unsupported "I am dedicated and detail-oriented" is weak; the same claim shown
  through a specific episode is strong.

**Move 3 — Reasons for Applying**
- *Gap in background*: candidly name a skill/knowledge gap. This is not an
  admission of weakness; it sets up the reason the program is needed.
- *Positive gains (incl. interests)*: how this program fills that gap and matches
  the applicant's interests. This is usually the most developed step in the move.
- *Program / university attributes*: specific, verifiable strengths of *this*
  program (faculty, curriculum, labs, pedagogy, links). Generic praise is worth
  nothing here.
- *Disciplinary / research reasons*: the intellectual/field-level motivation.

**Move 4 — Extra-curricular information** (optional)
- Include only if it speaks to suitability for graduate work.

**Move 5 — Conclusion**
- *Goals / prediction of future*: one clear forward direction.
- *Self-evaluation*: a brief, earned summative statement of fit.
- *Polite close*: a short, professional sign-off (thanks / forward-looking
  line). Keep it brief.

## 2. How the moves are actually sequenced

These are the findings that separate a researched SOP from a template:

- **Credentials (Move 2) and Reasons (Move 3) interweave.** In strong SOPs they
  are braided, not stacked as two separate blocks. A credential is introduced
  and immediately pointed at why the program is the logical next step. Aim for
  paragraphs that carry a past-fact and its future-implication together.
- **The Gap → Positive Gains sequence recurs.** Applicants move from their
  education/work background, to a named gap, to how the program closes it. Use
  this as the engine of the Reasons move.
- **Order is otherwise non-linear.** The Introduction's "goals/decision to apply"
  step often surfaces in the second or third paragraph; background gets
  interspersed. **Only the Conclusion reliably comes last.** Do not impose a
  rigid five-paragraph order.
- **Narrative runs through every move except the Conclusion.** Background,
  Education, Work, and Reasons are all best delivered as connected story —
  experiences causally linked to show development — rather than as a list. The
  Conclusion alone is expository: a direct statement of goals.

## 3. The three appeals, and how to weight them

**Ethos (credibility) — dominant, and established first.**
This is the single most important appeal in the SOP genre. Establish it early,
because reasoning and feeling do not persuade a reader who does not yet find the
applicant credible. Ethos is built through demonstrated competence: real
experience, specific accomplishments, and visible command of the field's
concerns. Professional affiliations and academic track record are the primary
material.

**Logos (reasoning/evidence) — to make ethos concrete.**
Logos shows a "believable self" by demonstrating knowledge of the discipline's
values and by backing every claim with specifics. If the applicant explains a
weaker grade, a transition, or a choice, that explanation is logos. Every "I am
interested in X" should be evidenced by something the applicant actually did.

**Pathos (emotion) — sparing, and bounded to one job.**
Pathos exists to surface the *personal, human dimension that transcripts and
recommendation letters cannot convey* — the genuine origin of an interest, the
character behind the record. It is not for sentimentality or inspirational
filler. A little, well-placed and specific, is persuasive; a lot reads as
manipulation and undercuts ethos.

Practical weighting: open and build on ethos; evidence everything with logos;
use one or two genuine pathos beats (often in the opening moment and the
motivation) and no more.

## 4. Self-construction

Successful applicants do not assert a single flat "I am qualified." They
construct several coherent **selves**, each *shown* through a specific moment:

- **Relevant self** — the applicant's background (even an unexpected one)
  connects naturally to the target field. Essential for career-changers: argue
  the connection explicitly and specifically; do not apologize for it.
- **Professional / credible self** — experience and competence; the ethos core.
- **Aspirant self** — honestly owns a gap and shows hunger to close it. Reads as
  intellectual maturity. This is where the Gap step lives.
- **Personal self** — the human behind the record; where the bounded pathos sits.
- **Benevolent self** (fellowships especially) — genuine intent to contribute /
  give back. Use only where it is real and the program values it.
- **Humble self** — a brief, gracious close.

The art is coherence: these should read as facets of one person, threaded
through the narrative, not as separate paragraphs labelled by trait.

## Quick assembly heuristic (taught master's as example — adapt for use case)

A workable spine, shown for a taught master's. For a research/PhD statement,
the same spine applies but the program-fit block centres on named supervisors
and research questions rather than curriculum. For a fellowship, the gains step
maps onto mission alignment with the funder's criteria.

Not a rigid template — adapt freely:

1. **Open** on the specific motivating moment (Generalization + a pathos beat).
2. **Build the relevant + professional self** by braiding Education/Work
   credentials with why each points toward this field (Move 2 ⟷ Move 3).
3. **Name the gap** candidly, then **the gains** this program provides
   (Gap → Positive Gains), anchored in **specific program attributes**.
4. **Close** with one clear career direction and a brief earned self-evaluation
   (Conclusion — expository, forward-looking).
