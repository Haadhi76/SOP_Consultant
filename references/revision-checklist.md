# Revision checklist

Use this to revise a draft, or to critique a draft the applicant brings. Work
top to bottom; the early items are the highest-yield. For each, the failure mode
is in parentheses.

## Structure and substance

- [ ] **Opens on a specific moment**, not a grand abstraction. (Failure: "Since
      childhood I have been fascinated by…")
- [ ] **Ethos is established early.** The reader has a reason to trust the
      applicant before any appeal to passion or reasoning. (Failure: feeling and
      ambition arrive before any evidence of competence.)
- [ ] **Credentials and reasons are interwoven**, not stacked as a "past" block
      then a "future" block. (Failure: three paragraphs of résumé, then a pivot
      to "that is why I want to apply.")
- [ ] **The Gap → Gains move is present**: a candid gap, then how *this* program
      fills it. (Failure: no stated reason the program is actually needed.)
- [ ] **Every claim is specific.** Each "I'm interested in / good at X" is backed
      by something the applicant did. (Failure: adjectives without evidence.)
- [ ] **The fit section is about *this* program** — named faculty, courses,
      labs, or features. Could it be pasted into another application unchanged?
      If yes, it fails. (Failure: generic praise of "rigor" and "reputation.")
- [ ] **No fabrication.** Every named person, lab, result, and credential is
      real and verifiable. (Failure: an invented professor or inflated result.)
- [ ] **Does not duplicate the CV.** It adds what transcripts and letters cannot
      show. Anything that just re-lists the résumé is cut. (Failure: prose CV.)
- [ ] **Coherent selves.** A relevant self, a credible self, and (for
      career-changers) an honestly-owned aspirant self come through — as facets
      of one person. (Failure: a flat "I am qualified" with no texture.)
- [ ] **One clear closing direction.** A single forward path, mapped through the
      program. (Failure: a hedged list of possible futures.)
- [ ] **Answers the program's actual prompt**, literally, if one was given.

## Voice and style

- [ ] **First person, active voice** dominate.
- [ ] **Contractions are rare**, not absent, not frequent.
- [ ] **Sentence length varies** because the content does — not uniformly long,
      not uniformly short.
- [ ] **No AI-vocabulary tells**: delve, tapestry, testament, realm, leverage,
      foster, navigate (figurative), multifaceted, robust, myriad, etc.
- [ ] **No formula sentences**: the "not just X but Y" tic, hollow aphorisms,
      relentless balanced triads.
- [ ] **Reads in the applicant's voice**, not a generic polished register. If a
      sample was provided, it matches.
- [ ] **Within the word limit.**

## Integrity

- [ ] The statement is the **applicant's own truthful story**, structured and
      sharpened — not invented for them.
- [ ] Self-presentation is **honest**: strengths argued, gaps contextualized,
      nothing misrepresented.
- [ ] No techniques here are being used to **disguise authorship or defeat
      AI-detection**. Where the program permits AI assistance, the applicant
      follows that program's disclosure rules.

## How to deliver a critique

When critiquing a draft the applicant brings, lead with what works, then give
the two or three highest-impact fixes (usually: more specificity, real program
fit, and cutting the generic register). Quote the applicant's own lines when
suggesting a rewrite, so the result stays in their voice.
