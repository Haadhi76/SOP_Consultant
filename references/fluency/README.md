# English-fluency levels — router

The SOP genre research is largely an English-for-Academic-Purposes literature:
much of it studies second-language (L2) applicants writing in English, because
that is where the genre's difficulty concentrates. These files adjust **how much
language support the SOP work needs and what to watch for** — they do **not**
change the applicant's substance, ideas, or honesty.

After identifying the applicant's comfort with academic English during intake,
read the matching file **in addition to** the core and use-case files.

| Level | File | Read it when… |
|---|---|---|
| Fluent / native | `fluent-or-native.md` | The applicant writes academic English comfortably; needs rhetoric and specificity, not language repair. |
| Advanced L2 | `advanced-l2.md` | Strong, communicative English with occasional non-native markers (articles, prepositions, collocations, register). |
| Developing L2 | `developing-l2.md` | English is a clear effort; meaning sometimes obscured by structure or vocabulary; needs more scaffolding. |

How to place the applicant: ask directly ("how comfortable are you writing
academic English — very, mostly, or it's a real effort?"), or infer from a draft
or writing sample they provide. When unsure, start one level *more supportive*
and dial back.

## The principle that governs all three levels

Language support is legitimate and ordinary — it is what university writing
centres and EAP instructors provide. The line: **help the applicant express
their own ideas, experiences, and voice clearly in English.** The ideas and the
truth remain entirely theirs. This is not about manufacturing a more "fluent"
persona to mislead a reader about who wrote it or about the applicant's English
level where that level is itself being assessed; it is about clarity, not
disguise. (This mirrors the integrity stance in the main SKILL.md.)
