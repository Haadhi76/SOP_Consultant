# Style and voice

Read this before drafting. Two parts:

1. **Stylistic register** — the sentence- and word-level patterns observed in
   successful SOPs.
2. **Authenticity** — how to keep the writing in the applicant's own voice and
   out of the generic, formulaic register that flat AI prose falls into.

The register findings come from the stylistic analysis of successful SOPs in
*Xchanges* (2021) and Ganguly (2020).

---

## 1. Stylistic register

What admitted applicants actually did:

- **First person, heavily.** "I" / "my" carry the SOP. It is a personal genre;
  an impersonal, committee-report tone is wrong for it.
- **Active voice, heavily.** Active voice creates directness and ownership ("I
  built", "I noticed", "I led") — not "it was observed that". Directness is
  persuasive here.
- **Contractions: sparingly.** Not banned, but rare. A few keep it human; many
  tip it toward informal. The target is *formal but personal*, not stiff.
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Long sentences develop an argument or
  connect ideas; short sentences land a point, create emphasis, and read as
  direct and even polite. The *combination* is what creates engagement —
  uniform length (long or short) is the giveaway of flat writing.
- **Favor simple and loose sentences for clarity.** A "loose" sentence states
  its main point first, then adds detail. This keeps the reader oriented. Save
  complex/periodic constructions for occasional effect.
- **Structured, coherent paragraphs.** One idea per paragraph, with real
  transitions that track the applicant's development — not "Furthermore" /
  "Moreover" connective tissue between unrelated points.

## 2. Authenticity — staying out of the generic AI register

A weak SOP is not usually weak because of grammar. It is weak because it could
have been written by — or about — anyone. The job here is the opposite of
"sounding human in general": it is sounding like **this specific applicant**.
That comes from real, particular content in the applicant's own idiom. The
guidance below removes the tells of flat, generic prose so the applicant's
actual voice is what remains.

> Note on scope: this is about authenticity and writing quality. It is **not** a
> method for making AI-generated text evade detection, and it should not be used
> or described that way. The aim is that the applicant's own true statement
> reads as their own — because it is. See the Hard rules in SKILL.md.

### Cut the AI vocabulary tells

These words and constructions cluster in generic model output and read as
hollow. Replace them with concrete, specific language:

- Inflated abstractions: *delve, tapestry, testament, realm, landscape,
  navigate (figurative), underscore, multifaceted, intricate, pivotal, leverage,
  harness, foster, holistic, robust, myriad.*
- Throat-clearing intensifiers: *deeply passionate, profoundly, incredibly,
  truly, genuinely, fascinating journey.*
- Empty cohesion: *In today's world…, It is worth noting that…, Furthermore /
  Moreover* stacked between unrelated points.

The fix is almost never a synonym — it is a specific fact. Replace "I have a
deep passion for data" with the actual thing the applicant did that shows it.

### Kill the formula sentences

- **The antithesis formula**: "It's not just X — it's Y." / "Not only X, but
  also Y." Used once it's fine; as a tic it screams template. Cut most of them.
- **Hollow aphorisms**: lines that sound profound and say nothing ("Data is the
  language of the modern world"; "Engineering is, at its core, about people").
  Delete them. An SOP earns insight through specifics, not mottos.
- **Over-balanced pairs and triads**: relentless parallel structure ("the
  curiosity to question, the discipline to test, and the humility to learn")
  reads as decorative. Allow it rarely; never let it set the rhythm.

### Restore real rhythm and specificity

- **Burstiness, but honestly.** Real writing varies sentence length because the
  *content* varies — a dense explanatory sentence followed by a four-word
  punch. Do not manufacture randomness; let the ideas dictate the rhythm.
- **Anchor every paragraph in something only this applicant could write** — a
  named project, a specific dataset, a particular failure, a concrete number, a
  real moment. Specifics are simultaneously the strongest persuasion *and* the
  surest marker of a genuine voice.
- **Match the applicant's idiom.** If the applicant supplied a writing sample or
  a draft, mirror their natural register, vocabulary, and rhythm. Do not impose
  a uniform "polished" voice over theirs.

### A quick before/after

- Generic: "I am deeply passionate about leveraging data to navigate complex,
  multifaceted challenges and foster meaningful impact."
- Specific: "In my last role I spent most of my time in spreadsheets, and I kept
  noticing the same thing: decisions were being made on numbers nobody had
  checked. I wanted to be the person who checked them."

The second is shorter, plainer, in a real voice, and tells the reader something
true and particular. That is the target.

## Length and format

- Honor the program's stated limit exactly. If none is given, ~650–750 words is
  a sound default for a taught master's; research/PhD statements often run
  longer where the program allows.
- No headings, no bullet lists inside the SOP itself — it is continuous prose.
- One clear, confident closing direction. No hedging across multiple futures.
