# MBA / business school — what changes

An MBA application turns on **career goals and demonstrated leadership**, and it
is almost always a *set* of short, separately-prompted essays read together —
not one statement. Those two facts drive every delta below. The MBA essay is
also hard to model: successful examples are confidential, which Loudermilk
(2007) characterizes as an *occluded genre* — so applicants lean on borrowed
templates and generic ambition. The cure is the skill's constant: specific,
truthful material.

This is a degree case. Career-changers are a large share of MBA applicants, so
the career-change modifier (`references/use-case-career-change.md`) very often
applies — layer it on. If the seat is funded by a fellowship or scholarship,
layer `references/use-case-fellowship-scholarship.md` too.

## Ethos is built differently here

Credibility in an MBA essay rests on **professional track record, leadership,
and quantified impact** — not research competence (PhD), academic record
(taught master's), or artistic sensibility (MFA). Lead with what the applicant
has *done and led*: roles, scope, decisions, and results in concrete numbers
where honest ones exist. Business schools state plainly that they select for
leadership potential — and that leadership counts at any level, no title
required. A team rescued, a process changed, a colleague developed, or a
project owned all build ethos, even without a senior title.

## What to emphasize

- **Career goals — short-term and long-term, post-MBA.** The single most
  universal element across programs. Name a *specific* immediate post-MBA role
  (often function + industry, sometimes target employers) and a longer-term
  direction the short-term step builds toward. Practitioner guidance (GMAC) is
  consistent: goals should be specific, logical, and attainable, and should
  flow naturally from the applicant's existing experience — not arrive from
  nowhere.
- **Why MBA, why now, why this school.** The Reasons-for-Applying move is
  dominated by this triad. *Why MBA*: the capability, credential, or network
  the goal requires and that the current path cannot supply. *Why now*: why
  this is the right career moment. *Why this school*: specific, verifiable
  features — named courses, professors, experiential offerings, clubs,
  recruiting paths, the cohort — never generic "prestige" or "reputation."
- **Leadership and impact, shown through a specific episode.** Do not assert
  leadership; narrate one moment of it and what changed because of the
  applicant.
- **Contribution / fit, as a distinct thread.** Most programs ask, in a
  separate essay, what the applicant will *give* the community — not just get
  from it. Answer it concretely (a club they will build, an experience they
  bring to the cohort), grounded in something they have actually done.
- **Self-awareness and values, where the prompt invites it.** Several programs
  (the archetype is a "what matters most to you, and why" essay) reward genuine
  reflection on values and formative experience. This is where the personal
  self lives; it must be specific and true, not a performance of depth.

## What to de-emphasize

- **Academic / research framing.** The MBA trains practitioners and leaders.
  Heavy theory, research methods, or a scholarly register signals the wrong
  audience.
- **The résumé in prose.** The essays complement the CV and the short-answer
  fields, which already carry the quantified accomplishments. The essays carry
  the *reasoning, reflection, and motivation* the résumé cannot. Anything that
  merely re-lists the résumé is cut.
- **Vague, inflated ambition.** "I want to be a transformational leader making
  global impact" is the genre's signature failure. Specific, plausible, and
  attainable beats grand every time.

## The Gap → Positive Gains structure, MBA form

The base Gap → Positive Gains sequence becomes career-driven: the *gap* is the
specific capability, credential, function switch, or network the applicant's
stated goal requires and that their current trajectory cannot supply; the
*positive gains* are exactly how this program's curriculum, experiences, and
community close it. For career-changers, this sequence does double duty — it is
also the answer to "why is this pivot credible?" (see
`references/use-case-career-change.md`).

## Multiple prompts, read together

An MBA application is usually several short, specifically-prompted essays plus
short-answer goal fields, evaluated as a set.

- **Answer each prompt literally** and to its (often very tight) word limit. A
  50-word goal answer and a 250-word community essay are different jobs; do not
  blur them.
- **Coordinate across the set** so the essays reinforce one coherent person
  rather than repeating each other or the résumé.
- **Prompts change format year to year** — schools regularly restructure the
  number, length, and theme of their essays. Always work from the program's
  *current, official* prompt for this cycle; never assume last year's questions.
  If the applicant has not supplied the current prompts, ask for them or offer
  to look them up.

## Typical shape

- Not one essay but a *set* of short ones (frequently 250–650 words each, with
  some 50-word short answers). Tight limits are the norm — concision and
  specificity matter more than in a longer SOP.
- Continuous prose, first person. The voice should read as a capable
  professional, not an academic.
- A workable spine for the central goals essay: a specific motivating thread
  from the applicant's career → a concrete short- and long-term goal → the gap
  the goal requires the MBA to fill → why this program specifically → a
  committed, plausible forward statement. Adapt to each prompt; do not force
  this onto an essay that asks for values or contribution.

## Sources

**Genre research (structure of the admissions-essay genre):**
- Loudermilk, B. C. (2007). Occluded academic genres: An analysis of the MBA
  Thought Essay. *Journal of English for Academic Purposes, 6*(3), 190–205.
  https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jeap.2007.07.001 (the MBA essay as an *occluded*
  genre — successful models are confidential, so applicants cannot easily
  pattern-match; cited for that argument and the move-analysis method, not as a
  study of goals essays specifically).
- Samraj, B., & Monk, L. (2008). The statement of purpose in graduate program
  applications: Genre structure and disciplinary variation. *English for
  Specific Purposes, 27*(2), 193–211. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esp.2007.07.001
  (one of its three corpora is a Business Administration master's program; the
  disciplines share moves but differ in the steps that realize them).
- Ding, H. (2007). Genre analysis of personal statements: Analysis of moves in
  application essays to medical and dental schools. *English for Specific
  Purposes, 26*(3), 368–392. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esp.2006.09.004
  (foundational five-move model for admissions personal statements — reason,
  credentials, experience, career goals, personality; medical/dental corpus,
  transferable, not business-specific).
- Bhatia, V. K. (1993). *Analysing Genre: Language Use in Professional
  Settings*. Longman (theoretical grounding: application / admissions essays as
  a promotional genre).

**Note on the evidence base:** No peer-reviewed genre study dedicated to the
modern MBA career-goals / "why-MBA" admissions essay appears to exist. The
structural guidance above adapts the general admissions-essay genre research;
the MBA-specific emphases (career goals, leadership, community contribution,
"why now") are grounded in the practitioner convention below.

**Practitioner convention (career-goals focus, leadership, fit, declarative
goals):** official admissions guidance from GMAC (mba.com / gmac.com) and the
published evaluation criteria and essay prompts of major MBA programs (e.g.,
Harvard Business School, Stanford GSB, Wharton, Chicago Booth, Kellogg, MIT
Sloan, Columbia) — convention and primary-source criteria, not empirical
research.
