# Career change — what changes

This is a **modifier**, not a standalone case. Layer it on top of
`references/use-case-taught-masters.md` or `references/use-case-research-phd.md`, whichever matches the target program.
It addresses the one problem a career-changer's SOP must solve: making a pivot
read as *continuity and considered intent*, not rupture or escape.

## The core move: build the relevant self

The base framework's "self-construction" becomes the whole game here. The
applicant must construct a **relevant self** — a version of themselves in which
the prior field and the target field are connected by a through-line, not
separated by a wall. Chiu (2016) shows that statement writers actively construct
identity rather than merely report a history; a career-changer's central task is
to construct an identity the pivot makes sense *for*.

Argue the connection explicitly. Do not assume the committee will infer that
prior experience transfers — name the transferable capability and show it with a
specific episode.

## Reframe the Gap step

In the base, the Gap-in-Background step names a skill deficit. For a
career-changer it does double duty: it is also where the SOP answers the unspoken
question, *why leave the current field?* Frame the gap as a **pull toward the
new field**, grounded in a genuine, evolving interest, rather than a **push away
from the old one** (dissatisfaction, burnout, money). The former reads as
direction; the latter reads as flight.

## Plausibility is the test (admissions-practitioner consensus)

Beyond the genre research, there is strong, consistent practitioner guidance on
career-change essays (most developed in the MBA-admissions literature, which is
dominated by career-changers). Treat the following as practitioner convention,
not empirical research:

- **Tell the story of an evolving understanding** of the target field — the
  moment(s) the interest formed and was tested through real action (a project, a
  side role, self-study, a conversation that changed the applicant's view).
- **Don't narrate a full career chronology.** Select one or two defining
  experiences that *created the gap the program will fill*. The goal is
  credibility and identity, not a prose résumé.
- **Make the path plausible.** Short-term and long-term goals should follow
  logically from each other and from the applicant's background. The reader
  should be able to trace an easy line from where the applicant is to where they
  say they are going.
- **Show evidence of commitment already.** The strongest career-change SOPs
  prove the pivot is underway (a course completed, a project shipped, a
  volunteer role taken) rather than merely intended.

## Tone

Do not over-apologize for the background. A non-traditional path, argued well, is
an asset — a source of perspective the target field lacks. Confidence about the
connection is part of the persuasion. (See `references/style-and-voice.md`.)
