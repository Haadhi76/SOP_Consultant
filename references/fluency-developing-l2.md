# Developing L2 — what changes

English is a clear effort for the applicant, and structure or vocabulary
sometimes obscures the meaning. Prioritize clarity and structure heavily, and
work closely from the applicant's own supplied material so the result stays
truthful and theirs.

## Clarity over flourish — and that is on-genre

The genre's preferred register is already plain: the research finds successful
statements favor **simple and loose sentences** for clarity and lucidity (see
`style-and-voice.md`). So writing clearly and plainly is not a compromise at
this level — it *is* the target register. Lean into it:

- Prefer short and medium sentences, one idea each, in the active voice.
- Choose common, precise words over rare or ornamental ones.
- Signpost the structure clearly so a reader follows the logic effortlessly.

## Scaffold structure first, language second

Build the SOP move by move from the applicant's real content:

1. Gather the concrete material (experiences, projects, the gap, the goal, the
   specific program facts) in whatever language mix the applicant is comfortable
   with.
2. Lay out the move-by-move outline and confirm the *content* with the applicant
   before polishing the English — so meaning is right before form.
3. Then render it in clear, correct English, preserving the applicant's actual
   points and emphasis.

Doing content before form protects against the most serious failure: fluent-
sounding English that quietly drifts from what the applicant actually meant.

## Self-promotion and self-disclosure

This genre is self-promotional, which is hard for first-time and L2 writers,
especially from cultures where modesty is the norm. Coach the applicant to make
direct, evidenced competence claims, and check that the personal/motivation
material genuinely comes from them — the reflective content is exactly what a
reader values and what cannot be borrowed.

## Integrity at this level (read carefully)

Heavy language support is legitimate and normal — it is what an EAP tutor or
writing centre does. The boundary:

- **The ideas, experiences, motivations, and choices must be the applicant's
  own.** Help them say *their* thing clearly; do not invent content to fill
  gaps.
- **Do not misrepresent the applicant's English proficiency where that
  proficiency is itself being assessed.** If a program evaluates language ability
  through the statement, or requires the work to be substantially the
  applicant's own writing, support has limits — be transparent with the
  applicant about that, and keep the help at the level of a writing tutor, not a
  ghostwriter.
- This mirrors the main SKILL.md hard rules: no fabrication, the statement is
  the applicant's own, no disguising authorship.
